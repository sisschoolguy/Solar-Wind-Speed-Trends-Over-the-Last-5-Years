Solar Wind Speed Trends Over the Last 5 Years (2020–2024)

By Mehdi Rahimli

Source: OMNIWeb

Description of Trends

This research report conducts an analysis of the variation in solar wind speed for the five-year period between January 2020 and December 2024. The data used comes from NASA's OMNIWeb service, based on monthly averages of solar wind speed (in km/s). 60 data points were used to create a time-series line graph, highlighting the trends and variations over this period.

The plot shows a pattern of monthly solar wind velocities ranging from some 330 km/s to approximately 500 km/s, with occasional spikes above 500 km/s. For 2020, the wind velocities were on average lower, typically between 330 km/s and 400 km/s, and thus imply a typically quiet solar wind year. Between 2021 and 2022, there is a consistent increase in solar wind speed with some months as high as 450 km/s.

By mid-22, the solar wind velocity displayed more extreme variability, like numerous abrupt increases. This might be indicative of periods of heightened solar activity, possibly associated with solar flares or coronal mass ejections (CMEs). For 2023, the graph registered some of its most elevated readings over the 5-year period, like a major spike at approximately 500 km/s. After this peak, the wind speeds are relatively very high in early 2024 with fluctuating ups and downs before decreasing again somewhat at the end of 2024.

The overall trend shows a consistent increase in average solar wind speed between 2020 and 2023, with a decline in 2024. However, within this trend, there are many irregular fluctuations, suggesting that solar wind speed is controlled by both long-term solar cycles and short-term solar activity.

Scientific Explanation

The trend in solar wind speed over the past five years can be explained mostly by the 11-year solar cycle, which regulates the increase and decrease of solar activity, including sunspots, solar flares, and CMEs. Solar Cycle 25 started in December 2019, according to NASA, and has been increasing in activity throughout the early 2020s.

The sluggish winds during 2020 and early 2021 are consistent with the solar minimum period, when the Sun is at its least active. As the cycle progressed into 2022 and 2023, the solar activity increased, tending to generate more turbulence in the solar atmosphere and faster solar wind streams. These high-energy events are the cause of sudden increases in wind speed, like those depicted by the peaks in the graph.

Moreover, the random sequence of peaks and troughs may be the result of high-speed solar wind streams originating in coronal holes within the Sun's surface, or because of the occurrence of transient solar storms. These variations indicate the dynamic and complex character of space weather.

Conclusion

The storyline of average solar wind speed during 2020-2024 shows a clear trend of increasing speed in the early part of Solar Cycle 25 with a peak in 2023. This trend is consistent with forecasted increase in solar activity in the middle-phase cycle. The dips and peaks point to the fact that solar wind not only follows the large-scale cycle but also short-scale solar events.

An understanding of solar wind activity is required to forecast space weather effects on Earth, such as geomagnetic storms disrupting satellites, communication systems, and power grids. Continuous monitoring will be essential as Solar Cycle 25 moves towards its anticipated peak around 2025.
